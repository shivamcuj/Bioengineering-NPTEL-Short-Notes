This lecture, titled **"Cell Cycle Disregulation and Cancer"** and delivered by Prof. Sanjeeva Srivastava at IIT Bombay, explores the mechanisms of cell division and how their failure leads to cancerous growth.

### Core Role of Cell Division

Life originates from a single cell—the fusion of a sperm and egg—and continues through regulated cell division. This process is essential for:

  * **Proliferation**: Distributing the mother cell's DNA (**chromosomes**, the structures containing genetic material) equally between two daughter cells.
  * **Biological Functions**: Facilitating growth, tissue repair, and reproduction.

### Cellular Organization of Genetic Material

A **genome** is the complete set of DNA molecules in a cell.

  * **Somatic Cells**: Normal, non-reproductive body cells that contain two sets of chromosomes.
  * **Gametes**: Reproductive cells (sperm and egg) that have half the number of chromosomes found in somatic cells.
  * **Nucleosomes**: Complexes formed by DNA tightly packing around eight **histone proteins**.
  * **Chromatin**: Fibers made of packed nucleosomes that condense into discrete chromosomes before division.

### The Cell Cycle

The cell cycle consists of the **Mitotic (M) phase**, where division occurs, alternating with a much longer growth period called **Interphase**, which accounts for approximately 90% of the cycle.

#### 1\. Interphase

Interphase prepares the cell for division through three sub-phases:

  * **G1 Phase**: The first growth part of interphase where nucleic acid and chromatin content are prepared.
  * **S Phase**: The phase where DNA replication occurs and chromosomes are duplicated.
  * **G2 Phase**: The final part of interphase, ensuring DNA replication is correct before entering the M phase.

#### 2\. Mitotic Phase

The M phase distributes chromosomes to daughter nuclei and involves two distinct processes: **Mitosis** (nuclear division) and **Cytokinesis** (division of the cytoplasm to produce two daughter cells).

### Comparison: Mitosis vs. Meiosis

| Feature         | Mitosis                       | Meiosis                            |
| :-------------- | :---------------------------- | :--------------------------------- |
| **Cell Type**   | Somatic cells                 | Gamete cells (Sperm/Ova)           |
| **Outcome**     | 2 identical daughter cells    | 4 unique daughter cells            |
| **DNA Content** | Same as mother cell           | Half the DNA of mother cell        |
| **Purpose**     | Growth, repair, replenishment | Reproduction and genetic variation |

**Meiosis** is unique because it introduces **genetic variability** through **crossing over** (at a site called the **chiasma**), where genes shuffle between maternal and paternal chromosomes.

### Regulation of the Cell Cycle

The cycle is governed by internal proteins that act as "traffic police," providing "stop" or "go-ahead" signals at specific **Checkpoints**.

  * **Checkpoints**:
      * **G1 Checkpoint**: Verifies parental DNA is intact before replication.
      * **G2 Checkpoint**: Ensures newly synthesized DNA is complete and intact before mitosis.
      * **M Checkpoint**: Confirms chromosomes are properly aligned at the **metaphase plate** (the center of the cell) before dividing.
  * **Regulatory Proteins**:
      * **Cyclins**: Proteins synthesized in the S phase that accumulate in the G2 phase.
      * **Cyclin-Dependent Kinases (CDKs)**: Enzymes expressed throughout the cycle that are only activated when bound to cyclins.

### External Regulation and Cancer

Healthy cells also respond to external signals:

  * **Density-Dependent Inhibition**: Crowded cells stop dividing when space is insufficient.
  * **Anchorage Dependence**: Cells must be attached to a specific substrate (environment) to grow.

**Cancer cells** dysregulate these processes by losing dependence on both internal and external signals. They do not stop at checkpoints, ignore density-dependent inhibition (forming multiple layers or tumor masses), and do not require anchorage to grow.

### Future Technologies in Cancer Detection

Current research focuses on detecting **Circulating Tumor Cells (CTCs)**.

  * **FDA-Cleared Technology**: Uses antibodies against **EPCAM** (**epithelial cell adhesion molecules**) coated with **ferrofluids** for sensitive capture of tumor cells.
  * **Approaches**: These include immunocytological technologies (protein-based) and molecular technologies (RNA-based) to identify tumor markers and exclude them from normal circulation.

#### **Short Answer Model Answers**

1.  Mitosis produces two daughter cells that are genetically identical to the mother cell and contain the same amount of DNA. In contrast, meiosis produces four unique daughter cells that contain only half the amount of DNA as the mother cell.
2.  The G2 checkpoint is the final part of interphase and serves to ensure that the newly synthesized DNA strands are complete and intact before the cell is allowed to proceed into the mitotic phase.
3.  CDKs are expressed continuously throughout the cell cycle but remain inactive on their own. They only become activated after binding with cyclins, which are specifically synthesized during the S phase and accumulate during the G2 phase.
4.  Normal cells require nutrients, growth factors, and specific space (density) or substrates (anchorage) to divide. Cancer cells lose dependence on these signals; they do not stop at checkpoints, they ignore density-dependent inhibition to form tumor masses, and they do not require anchorage to grow.
5.  CTCs are tumor cells that have detached from a primary tumor and entered the bloodstream. Detecting them is challenging because it requires highly sensitive technology to accurately identify and capture a very small number of tumor cells (sometimes one in a thousand) from the normal cell circulation.
