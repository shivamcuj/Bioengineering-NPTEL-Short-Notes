This document, titled **"Principles and Application of Animal Cloning"** (Lecture 28 of the course *Bioengineering: An Interface with Biology and Medicine*), explores the concepts of cell reprogramming and cloning in plants and animals. Its primary purpose is to explain how cells can be reprogrammed for production or therapeutic purposes while discussing the major scientific breakthroughs and scandals that have occurred in this field.

### 1\. Cloning of Plants

The document begins by explaining that plant cells have a high degree of flexibility regarding their developmental potential.

  * **Totipotency**: Mature plant cells can "dedifferentiate," meaning they revert from a specialized state to a more basic state that can give rise to all specialized cell types of an organism. A cell with this potential is described as **totipotent**.
  * **Plant Cloning Process**: Cloning can be achieved using root cells grown in culture. These cells dedifferentiate to form a **callus**, which is a mass of unspecialized plant cells.
  * **Hormonal Stimulation**: By stimulating the callus with plant hormones like **Cytokinin** and **Gibberellic acid**, it can form roots and shoots, eventually resulting in a full plant.
  * **Applications**: This is widely used in agriculture to reproduce plants with valuable characteristics, such as resistance to pathogens, and for the commercial production of plants like orchids.

### 2\. Animal Cloning: Nuclear Transplantation

Animal cloning is described as a more "tedious" and complex process compared to plant cloning.

  * **Nuclear Transplantation**: This procedure involves removing the nucleus of an unfertilized or fertilized egg (creating an **enucleated** egg) and replacing it with the nucleus of a differentiated (specialized) cell.
  * **Gurdon’s Experiment (1960s)**: Sir John B. Gurdon tested the totipotency of animal cells using frog eggs.
      * When he transplanted nuclei from an early embryo, most recipient eggs developed into tadpoles.
      * However, when he used nuclei from fully differentiated intestinal cells, less than 2% of the eggs developed into normal tadpoles.
      * This suggested that while animal cells can be reprogrammed, the success rate depends heavily on the stage of differentiation of the donor cell.

### 3\. Reproductive Cloning of Mammals

The document details the progression from early experiments to the cloning of large mammals.

  * **Dolly the Sheep (1996)**: Dr. Ian Wilmut achieved a major breakthrough by cloning the first large animal from **somatic cells** (normal body cells that are not reproductive cells).
  * **Mechanism**: Researchers took **mammary cells** (milk-producing cells) from one sheep and an egg cell from another. They removed the nucleus from the egg and fused it with the nucleus of the mammary cell, often using electric shock to facilitate the process.
  * **Surrogate Development**: The resulting embryo was transplanted into a third sheep, a **surrogate mother**, to ensure the cloned animal's DNA was clearly derived from the donor and not the birth mother.
  * **Limitations and Imperfections**: Cloned animals are not always perfect "carbon copies". For example, the **CC (Carbon Copy) Cat** did not have the same fur color as its genetic donor due to random phenomena like **X chromosome inactivation** and environmental factors.
  * **Health and Ethics**: Many cloned embryos develop abnormalities. Dolly herself died at age six, which is young for a sheep, leading to questions about whether the "age" of the donor DNA affects the clone's lifespan.

### 4\. Stem Cells and Therapeutic Interventions

Due to ethical concerns and low efficiency, reproductive cloning was banned in many places, shifting the focus toward **therapeutic cloning**.

  * **Embryonic Stem (ES) Cells**: These are unspecialized cells that can reproduce indefinitely and differentiate into specialized cells (like heart or nerve cells) under specific culture conditions.
  * **Induced Pluripotent Stem (iPS) Cells**: Dr. Shinya Yamanaka discovered a way to "deprogram" fully differentiated human cells (like skin fibroblasts) back into a stem-cell-like state.
  * **Transcription Factors**: Yamanaka used a retroviral vector to introduce four specific genes (**Oct3/4, Sox2, c-Myc, and Klf4**) into differentiated cells to acquire pluripotency. This research, alongside Gurdon's earlier work, won the Nobel Prize in 2012.

### 5\. Scientific Scandals and Ethics

The document concludes with a discussion on the importance of ethics in research, highlighting two major scandals.

  * **Dr. Hwang Woo-Suk**: Once a "cloning star" in South Korea, he claimed the first success in human somatic cell cloning. However, his work was found to be fraudulent, involving fabricated results and the unethical purchase of human eggs.
  * **Dr. Haruko Obokata**: She reported a phenomenon called **STAP (Stimulus-triggered Acquisition of Pluripotency)**, claiming that simply changing the pH of a medium could reprogram cells. The study was retracted after investigations found manipulated images and an inability to reproduce the results. This scandal led to severe punishments for the research team and the suicide of one researcher.
