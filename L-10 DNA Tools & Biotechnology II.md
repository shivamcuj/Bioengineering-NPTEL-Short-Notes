### **Part 1: Agarose Gel Electrophoresis**

This technique is used to visualize DNA and check if a specific DNA product has been successfully produced.

  * **Step 1: Preparation:** To make a 2% gel, weigh 2 grams of **agarose powder** and add it to 100mL of **TAE buffer** in a flask.
  * **Step 2: Dissolving:** Heat the mixture in a microwave for approximately 2 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the agarose is completely dissolved.
  * **Step 3: Adding EtBr:** Once the gel has cooled slightly, add **Ethidium Bromide (EtBr)** at a ratio of 0.5 microgram per ml. EtBr is a **potent mutagen** that **intercalates** with DNA, causing it to **fluoresce** (glow) under a **transilluminator**.
  * **Step 4: Casting:** Pour the liquid gel into a **casting unit**. Insert a **comb** into the gel before it solidifies to create **wells** where the DNA samples will be loaded.
  * **Step 5: Running the Gel:** Place the solidified gel into an **electrophoretic unit** (running unit) and immerse it in 1x TAE buffer.
  * **Step 6: Loading Samples:** Mix the DNA sample (e.g., 8 microliters of PCR product) with **loading dye**. Carefully load this mixture into the gel wells.
  * **Step 7: DNA Ladder:** Load a **DNA ladder** (e.g., a 100 base pair ladder) into one of the wells. This acts as a reference to determine the size of the DNA fragments in the samples.
  * **Step 8: Electrophoresis:** Connect the unit to a power supply and set the voltage (e.g., 100 volts for 1 hour). Bubbles will indicate the gel has started running.

### **Part 2: Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)**

PCR is a method used to **amplify** (create millions of copies of) a very small quantity of DNA.

  * **PCR Mixture Components:**
      * **Reaction Buffer:** Provides the optimal environment for the enzyme.
      * **Taq DNA Polymerase:** The enzyme responsible for synthesizing new DNA strands.
      * **Primers:** Short DNA sequences (Forward and Reverse) that mark the starting point for DNA synthesis.
      * **dNTPs:** The building blocks (nucleotides) used to build the new DNA strands.
      * **Nuclease-free water:** Used to bring the reaction to the required volume.
      * **Template DNA:** The original DNA sample to be amplified.
  * **The PCR Process (Thermal Cycling):**
    1.  **Initialization:** Activates the enzyme for the reaction.
    2.  **Denaturation (approx. 94°C):** High heat separates the double-stranded DNA into two single strands.
    3.  **Annealing (approx. 58°C):** The temperature is lowered so primers can bind (anneal) to their matching sequences on the single-stranded DNA.
    4.  **Extension (approx. 72°C):** Taq polymerase adds dNTPs to the primers to synthesize new DNA strands.
  * **Cycling:** These three steps (denaturation, annealing, and extension) are typically repeated for 30–35 cycles to achieve massive amplification.
  * **Final Steps:** A final extension at 72°C ensures all products are complete, followed by a "hold" at 4°C to cool the samples.

### **Part 3: Advanced PCR Techniques**

  * **Reverse Transcription PCR (RT-PCR):** Used when the starting material is **RNA**. The enzyme **reverse transcriptase** first converts the RNA into **complementary DNA (cDNA)**, which is then amplified using traditional PCR.
  * **Real-Time PCR (qPCR):** This technique measures the amount of DNA being produced *during* the reaction using a **fluorescent probe** with a **reporter** and a **quencher** molecule. As Taq polymerase synthesizes new DNA, its **5' to 3' exonuclease activity** degrades the probe, releasing the reporter from the quencher and causing fluorescence. The increase in fluorescence is directly indicative of the amount of DNA being synthesized and can be used to measure **gene expression**.
