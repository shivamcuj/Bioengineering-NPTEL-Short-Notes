The primary objective of this lecture is to examine deviations from Mendelian genetics, illustrating that not all inheritance patterns follow Mendel's simple laws of dominance.

### Incomplete Dominance

  * **Deviation from Mendel**: The lecture begins by contrasting Mendel's findings (e.g., purple vs. white flowers where purple was always dominant) with **Incomplete Dominance**, where alleles are not completely dominant or recessive.
  * **Red Snapdragons**: In this example, crossing a red flower ($C^RC^R$) with a white flower ($C^WC^W$) produces a hybrid $F\_1$ generation that is **Pink** ($C^RC^W$)—a color neither red nor white.
  * **Phenotypic Ratios**: When $F\_1$ pink hybrids are crossed, the $F\_2$ generation results in a ratio of **1:2:1** (1 red, 2 pink, and 1 white).
  * **Genotype vs. Phenotype**: In Mendelian inheritance, the 1:2:1 ratio typically describes the **genotype** (genetic makeup), while the phenotype (physical appearance) is 3:1. In incomplete dominance, the **phenotypic ratio** itself becomes 1:2:1.

### Albinism: A Recessively Inherited Disorder

  * **Definition**: Albinism is described as an inheritance pattern where a deficiency in coloration results in a lack of pigmentation and white hair.
  * **Homozygous Expression**: This trait is a **recessively inherited disorder**, meaning it only appears in **homozygous** individuals ($aa$)—those who possess two copies of the recessive allele.
  * **Carrier Status**: Individuals with a **heterozygous** genotype ($Aa$) are **carriers**; they appear normal (normal phenotype) but carry the recessive gene.
  * **Probability in Siblings**: If two carrier parents ($Aa \\times Aa$) have four children, there is a statistical possibility that one will be albino ($aa$), two will be carriers ($Aa$), and one will be homozygous normal ($AA$).

### Multiple Alleles and ABO Blood Grouping

  * **Multiple Alleles**: While Mendel studied genes with two alleles, some genes exist in more than two allelic forms, such as those determining human blood types.
  * **Blood Group Types**: There are four blood types—**A, B, AB, and O**—determined by the presence or absence of specific carbohydrates (antigens) on the surface of **RBCs** (Red Blood Cells).
  * **The Three Alleles**: The ABO system involves three alleles: $I^A$, $I^B$, and $i$.
      * **$I^A$ and $I^B$**: These are **co-dominant** alleles that produce carbohydrate A and carbohydrate B, respectively.
      * **$i$**: This is a recessive allele that produces no carbohydrate.
  * **Genotypes and Phenotypes**:
      * **Type A**: Genotypes $I^AI^A$ or $I^Ai$.
      * **Type B**: Genotypes $I^BI^B$ or $I^Bi$.
      * **Type AB**: Genotype $I^AI^B$ (illustrates co-dominance).
      * **Type O**: Genotype $ii$ (requires both recessive alleles).

### Laboratory Demonstration: Blood Typing

  * **Antigen-Antibody Interaction**: Blood typing is determined by observing reactions between blood antigens and specific antibodies.
  * **Agglutination**: If an antibody (e.g., Anti-A) reacts with its corresponding antigen (Antigen A), **agglutination** (clumping of particles) occurs.
  * **The Experiment**: Three drops of blood are tested with three different antibodies:
      * **Anti-A**: Reacts with blood groups A and AB.
      * **Anti-B**: Reacts with blood groups B and AB.
      * **Anti-D**: Used to determine the **Rh antigen** (Rhesus factor) to see if the blood is positive or negative.

### Genetic Problem Solving

The lecture concludes with complex problems involving independent inheritance and multiple traits:

  * **Problem 1**: If a Type A man and Type B woman have a Type O child, their genotypes must be $I^Ai$ and $I^Bi$ respectively.
  * **Problem 2**: This problem explores a recessively inherited disease expressed only in individuals with Type O blood, assuming both parents are heterozygous for the disease gene ($D$). Using a dihybrid cross ($AaDd \\times BbDd$), the probability of a child having both Type O blood and the disease is calculated as **1/16**.

### Summary of Principles

  * **Theory of Inheritance**: Gregor Mendel formulated this based on discrete "genes" transmitting characteristics.
  * **Diploid Nature**: Individuals contain two copies of genes, inheriting one from each parent.
  * **Hidden Traits**: A trait (like a recessive disorder) may not show up in an individual but can still be passed to the next generation.
