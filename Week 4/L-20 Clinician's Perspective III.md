The primary purpose of this session is to bridge the gap between engineering and medicine by providing a clinician's perspective on the challenges and emerging opportunities in diagnosing infectious diseases.

### **Introduction and Background**

  * **Instructor and Guest:** The course is led by **Prof. Sanjeeva Srivastava** from **IIT Bombay**, who introduces **Dr. Jayanthi Shastri**, a Professor and Head of Microbiology at **TNMC and BYL Nair Ch. Hospital**.
  * **Expertise:** Dr. Shastri specializes in infectious disease diagnosis, specifically validating rapid techniques for **Dengue**, **Malaria**, and **HIV**.
  * **Facility:** She commissioned a state-of-the-art **molecular diagnostic facility** in Mumbai, recognized by **NACO (National AIDS Control Organization)** as a **Regional HIV Reference laboratory**. This lab performs **real-time PCR** (a laboratory technique used to amplify and simultaneously detect a specific DNA molecule) for viral loads and **Early infant diagnosis** by **DNA PCR**.

### **Infectious Disease Diagnosis: Challenges & Opportunities**

  * **Current Lab Functions:** Dr. Shastri’s lab focuses on **acute febrile illnesses** (diseases characterized by a sudden onset of fever) such as dengue, malaria, typhoid, and hepatitis.
  * **Microbial Classification:** Microbes are described as "invisible invaders".
      * **Bacteria:** Classified as **Gram Positive** (containing more **peptidoglycan**, a polymer consisting of sugars and amino acids that forms a mesh-like layer outside the plasma membrane) or **Gram Negative** (containing less peptidoglycan and more lipids). This distinction is critical for selecting the correct antibiotics.
      * **Other Pathogens:** Include viruses (e.g., Dengue, Hepatitis), parasites (e.g., Malarial parasite, worms), and fungi (e.g., Candida).
  * **Diagnostic Methods:**
      * **Multiplex PCR:** A technique that targets multiple pathogens (bacteria, viruses, parasites) simultaneously in a single clinical sample, such as blood, stool, or urine.
      * **Antimicrobial Susceptibility Testing:** Conventional testing using **Muller-Hinton agar** (a microbiological growth medium) to determine if a bug is sensitive or resistant to drugs like **artemisinin** for malaria.
  * **The Rising Threat of Drug Resistance:** Antibiotic resistance occurs when a few resistant bacteria survive treatment, grow, and transfer their resistance to others.

### **The Need of the Hour: Point-of-Care (POC) Diagnostics**

  * **Core Challenge:** Clinicians need to know quickly if an infection is bacterial, viral, fungal, or parasitic to ensure the **rational use of antibiotics** and avoid "abuse" for non-bacterial infections.
  * **Current Limitations:** Technologies like **DNA sequencing** and **genomic analyzers** are highly accurate but too time-consuming, often taking days.
  * **Proposed Solution:** A **Lab-on-a-chip** for **POC diagnostics**. This would be a sensitive, specific, and low-cost test performed at the patient's bedside.
  * **Clinical Advantages:** Reducing **Turn-around time (TAT)** (the time from sample collection to result), enabling **evidence-based diagnosis**, and supporting **antibiotic stewardship** (the systematic effort to educate and persuade clinicians of the prescribed use of antibiotics).

### **Disease Progression and Biomarkers**

  * **Diagnostic Dilemmas:** Many seasonal diseases like Dengue, Malaria, and **Leptospirosis** present with similar symptoms such as fever and joint pains.
  * **Dengue Variability:** Infection can be self-limiting or progress to life-threatening **Dengue Hemorrhagic Fever (DHF)** or **Dengue Shock Syndrome (DSS)**.
  * **Biomarkers:** There is a need for identifying **biomarkers** (biological molecules found in blood or other body fluids that are a sign of a normal or abnormal process) using "omics" technologies—**transcriptomics**, **proteomics**, **genomics**, and **metabolomics**—to predict which patients will progress to severe disease.

### **Emerging Technologies and AI**

  * **Nanotechnology:** Offers potential for rapid identification of infectious agents and lower detection thresholds (e.g., detecting fewer than 50 copies of HIV).
  * **Emerging Infectious Diseases (EID):** New diseases like **SARS**, **MERS**, **Ebola**, and **Zika** are emerging due to evolutionary changes, modern transportation, and ecological shifts.
  * **Artificial Intelligence (AI):**
      * **Predictive Modeling:** Using **GIS (Geographic Information System) mapping** to identify "Hot Spots" and forecast the spread of diseases.
      * **Drug Discovery:** AI is being used to discover new molecules, such as those for neuroscience drugs.
      * **Clinical Care:** Apps and systems are being developed to detect **diabetic retinopathy**, suggest cancer treatment plans (**IBM**), and screen chest X-rays (**Enlitic**).
      * **Imaging:** AI can reduce the time to delineate tumor edges in **MRI** or **CT scans** from hours to seconds with total consistency.

### **Conclusion and Future Outlook**

  * **Cautionary Note:** Dr. Shastri notes that while most protein structures are studied in crystallized forms, real proteins are flexible, presenting a challenge for AI.
  * **Take-Home Message:** The future lies in creating affordable, high-quality, bedside diagnostics for developing countries like India. AI will eventually "crack open the inner workings of a cell".
