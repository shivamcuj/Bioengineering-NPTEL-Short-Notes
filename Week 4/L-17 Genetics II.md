### Introduction to Mendelian Genetics in Humans

  * **Mendelian Foundations**: Mendel’s original rules were based on limited conditions in pea plants, typically involving one gene with two **alleles** (alternative versions of a gene) or two genes with four alleles.
  * **Human Complexity**: Many human characteristics are **polygenic**, meaning they are governed by multiple genes, making their inheritance more complex than the simple patterns predicted by Mendel.
  * **Pedigree Analysis**: This is a method of family history analysis used to track a particular **trait** (a specific characteristic) across generations. By assembling a family tree, researchers can see how traits appear, disappear, and reappear.

### Human Traits Following Mendelian Patterns

  * **Widow's Peak**: This is a sharp, V-shaped hairline that is a **dominant trait**, meaning it only requires one copy of the allele to be expressed.
  * **Attached Earlobe**: In contrast, an attached earlobe is a **recessive trait**, meaning it is only expressed when an individual has two copies of that specific allele.
  * **Polydactyly**: This is a condition where a child is born with extra fingers or toes. Interestingly, while polydactyly is a dominant trait, the recessive allele (having five digits) is far more prevalent in the population.

### Degrees of Dominance

Inheritance is not always a matter of one allele being completely "stronger" than another. The document defines three levels:

  * **Complete Dominance**: The **phenotype** (observable physical trait) of a **heterozygote** (an individual with two different alleles) is identical to that of a dominant **homozygote** (an individual with two identical dominant alleles).
  * **Incomplete Dominance**: The phenotype of the first-generation (**F1**) hybrids is a blend, falling somewhere between the two parental varieties.
  * **Co-dominance**: Two dominant alleles affect the phenotype in separate, distinguishable ways.

### Problem-Solving and Genetic Principles

The lecture transitions into solving genetics problems using various tools and laws:

  * **Punnett Square**: A diagram used to predict the **genotypic** (the genetic makeup) and phenotypic outcomes of a cross.
  * **Law of Segregation**: This law suggests that the two alleles for a heritable trait separate from each other during **gamete** (reproductive cell) formation.
  * **Law of Independent Assortment**: This states that each pair of alleles for different genes segregates independently during gamete formation.

#### Case Study: Complex Cross (Seed Shape and Flower Color)

The lecture explores a cross involving **incomplete dominance** (red RR x white rr = pink Rr) and seed shape:

  * **F1 Generation**: A cross between a true-breeding round-red plant and a wrinkled-white plant results in offspring that all have round seeds and pink flowers.
  * **F2 Generation**: Crossing these F1 hybrids leads to a complex phenotypic ratio of **6:3:3:2:1:1**, including variations like round seeds with red flowers and wrinkled seeds with pink flowers.

#### Case Study: Vicia ervilia (Bitter Vetch) Height

In another example, short stature (S) is dominant over long stature (s):

  * **Genotype Identification**: A long plant must have the genotype 'ss' because long stature is recessive.
  * **Parental Analysis**: A long plant ('ss') could have had a parent with a short phenotype if that parent was a heterozygote ('Ss').
  * **Test Cross Results**: Crossing a long plant ('ss') with a short, bushy neighbor's plant that results in half-short and half-long offspring proves the neighbor's plant had the genotype 'Ss'.

### Short Answer Model Answers

1.  **Pedigree analysis** is a family history analysis for a specific trait where information is assembled into a family tree to describe the traits of parents and children across generations.
2.  **Incomplete dominance** results in an F1 hybrid phenotype that is a blend between the two parental varieties, whereas **co-dominance** involves two dominant alleles that affect the phenotype in separate, distinguishable ways.
3.  The **Law of Independent Assortment** states that two or more genes assort independently, meaning each pair of alleles segregates independently of other pairs during gamete formation.
4.  The **frequency of an allele** is not determined by whether it is dominant; for example, while polydactyly (extra digits) is a dominant trait, the recessive allele for five digits is far more prevalent in the human population.
5.  The genotype of the short parent must be **heterozygous (Ss)**, because a cross with a recessive long plant (ss) that produces long offspring (ss) requires the short parent to contribute a recessive 's' allele.
