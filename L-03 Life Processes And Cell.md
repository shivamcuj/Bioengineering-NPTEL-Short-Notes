This lecture, titled "**Life Processes & Cell**," is the third installment of the MOOC NPTEL course "Bioengineering: An Interface with Biology and Medicine," delivered by Prof. Sanjeeva Srivastava from IIT Bombay. The session focuses on defining life, identifying its fundamental properties, and exploring the basic structural unit of all living systems: the cell.

### Properties and Processes of Life

The lecture identifies seven key properties that characterize living organisms, using specific examples to illustrate each:

  * **Energy Processing**: Living things obtain and use chemical energy from food to power their activities, such as a butterfly getting energy from flower nectar or a hummingbird powering its flight.
  * **Order**: Life is characterized by highly ordered, symmetrical structures, exemplified by the intricate pattern of a sunflower.
  * **Regulation**: Organisms have mechanisms to maintain a constant internal environment. For example, a Jack Rabbit regulates its body temperature by exchanging heat through its ears.
  * **Evolutionary Adaptation**: Traits that are best suited to an environment evolve over generations and become hereditary. The pygmy seahorse's ability to camouflage itself is a prime example of this.
  * **Growth and Development**: Inherited genetic information directs the growth and development of organisms, as seen in an oak seedling.
  * **Response to Environment**: Organisms make immediate reactions to environmental stimuli. For instance, a Venus flytrap rapidly closes its trap when a damselfly lands on it.
  * **Reproduction**: The process of producing offspring is essential for the continuation of life, illustrated by a mother giraffe and her baby.

### Unifying Themes of Life

Despite the diversity of life, several common themes connect all organisms:

  * **Organization**: Biological organization occurs at multiple levels: biosphere, ecosystem, community, population, organism, organs, tissue, cell, organelle, and molecules.
  * **Information Flow (Central Dogma)**: Genetic information is transmitted from one generation to the next. The "central dogma" describes how information flows from **DNA** (transcription) to **RNA** and then to **proteins** (translation).
  * **Energy and Matter**: Life depends on the transfer of energy and matter through ecosystems, involving intricate chemical cycling.
  * **Interactions**: Interactions between organisms and their environment, as well as between biomolecules within a cell, are crucial for biological processes.
  * **Evolution**: This is the central theme of biology, explaining both the diversity of life and the remarkable biochemical commonalities shared by all organisms, from bacteria to humans.

### The Cell: The Basic Unit of Life

The cell is defined as the fundamental structural and functional unit of life and the simplest collection of matter that can be alive.

#### Studying Cells via Microscopy

Because most cells are between 1-100 µm in diameter, advanced microscopy is required to study them:

  * **Light Microscopy**: Used to image live cells and their general structures. Variants include brightfield, phase-contrast, fluorescence, confocal, and super-resolution microscopy.
  * **Electron Microscopy**: Used for viewing ultra-structural details of dead, fixed cells. Two main types are **Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM)** and **Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM)**.

#### Types of Cells

  * **Prokaryotic Cells**: "Pro" (before) and "karyon" (nucleus) indicates these cells lack a true, membrane-enclosed nucleus. Their DNA is located in a diffused region called the **nucleoid**. Examples include bacteria like *E. coli*.
  * **Eukaryotic Cells**: "Eu" (true) indicates these cells have a distinct, membrane-bound nucleus and other specialized organelles.
      * **Animal Cells**: Contain organelles like mitochondria, Golgi apparatus, endoplasmic reticulum (rough and smooth), lysosomes, and centrosomes.
      * **Plant Cells**: Share many organelles with animal cells but have unique features like a **cell wall**, **chloroplasts** for photosynthesis, **plasmodesmata**, and a large **central vacuole**.

### The Three Domains of Life

Modern classification divides life into three fundamental groups called domains based on biochemical characteristics and DNA sequences:

1.  **Bacteria**: Common prokaryotes such as *Salmonella* and *Bacillus*.
2.  **Archaea**: Prokaryotes that often live in extreme environments (e.g., **thermophiles** in high heat, **halophiles** in high salt, or **methanogens** in methane-rich areas). Though prokaryotic, they share many similarities with eukaryotes, such as having **histone proteins** and similar ribosomal RNA.
3.  **Eukarya**: All eukaryotic organisms, including fungi, plants (*Zea mays*), and animals (*Homo sapiens*).

The lecture concludes by noting that all life likely diverged from a **common ancestor**, supported by the uniformity of metabolic pathways and the use of the same 20 amino acids across all domains.

**Short Answer Solutions**

1.  A cell is the **structural and functional unit of life**, the basic unit of structure and function in an organism, and the simplest collection of matter that can be alive.
2.  **Light microscopy** allows for the imaging of **live cells**, whereas **electron microscopy** can only be used to study **dead cells** because the cells must be fixed and sectioned to view ultra-structural details.
3.  Unifying themes include **Organization** (levels from biosphere to molecules), **Information** (genetic transmission/central dogma), **Energy and Matter** (transfer and flow), **Interactions** (between organisms or biomolecules), and **Evolution**.
4.  The **nucleoid** is a diffused region in the cytoplasm of a prokaryotic cell where the bacterial chromosome (DNA) is located; it is **not enclosed** by a nuclear membrane.
5.  By observing bacteria (*Thermus aquaticus*) that survive in **hot springs**, Kary Mullis isolated the **Taq polymerase** enzyme. Because this enzyme can withstand high temperatures, it became the fundamental component for the **Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)**.
