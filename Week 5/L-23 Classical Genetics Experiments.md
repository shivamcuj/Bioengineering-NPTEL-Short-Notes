Its primary purpose is to explain foundational experiments in molecular biology that identified DNA as the genetic material and demonstrated how it replicates.

### Introduction to Personalized Medicine

  * The lecture begins by highlighting how modern biotechnology tools and an understanding of genome variation (differences in the DNA sequence among individuals) enable **personalized medicine**.
  * This approach uses treatments tailored to an individual's specific mutations (changes in DNA), which can be more effective and have fewer side effects than generic drugs.

### Griffith's Experiment (1928): The Transforming Principle

This section explains how genetic traits can be transferred between different bacterial strains using *Streptococcus pneumoniae*.

  * **Strains**: The experiment used two strains: the **S strain (Smooth)**, which has a protective capsule and causes pneumonia (pathogenic), and the **R strain (Rough)**, which lacks a capsule and does not cause pneumonia (nonpathogenic).
  * **Experimental Conditions and Results**:
      * Injecting living S cells killed the mouse.
      * Injecting living R cells or heat-killed S cells left the mouse healthy.
      * Injecting a mixture of **heat-killed S cells and living R cells** killed the mouse.
  * **Conclusion**: Griffith concluded that a **Transforming principle/factor** (later identified as DNA) moved from the dead S cells to the living R cells, transforming them into pathogenic cells.

### Hershey and Chase Experiment: DNA vs. Protein

This experiment addressed whether DNA or protein was the genetic material using **T2 phages** (viruses that infect bacteria, known as bacteriophages).

  * **Labeling Strategy**:
      * **32 Phosphorus ($^{32}P$)** was used to label DNA, as phosphorus is part of the DNA backbone.
      * **35 Sulfur ($^{35}S$)** was used to label proteins, specifically targeting methionine residues (an amino acid containing sulfur).
  * **Methodology**: After infection, a **centrifuge** (a machine that spins samples at high speeds to separate components) was used. The heavy bacterial cells formed a **pellet** at the bottom, while the lighter liquid remained as the **supernatant**.
  * **Results**: Radioactivity from DNA was found in the pellet (inside the bacteria), while protein radioactivity remained in the supernatant.
  * **Conclusion**: DNA, not protein, is the genetic material that enters cells and carries hereditary information.

### Chemical Composition of DNA

  * **Structure**: DNA is a double helix illustrated by **Watson and Crick**, who received the Nobel Prize in 1962.
  * **Base Pairing**: It consists of complementary base pairs: **Adenine (A)** pairs with **Thymine (T)** via two hydrogen bonds, and **Guanine (G)** pairs with **Cytosine (C)** via three hydrogen bonds.
  * **Chargaff's Rule**: Derived by Erwin Chargaff, this rule states that the percentage of A equals the percentage of T, and the percentage of G equals the percentage of C.

### DNA Replication Models

Three hypotheses were proposed for how DNA makes copies of itself:

  * **Conservative Model**: The original two parental strands remain together, acting as a template for a completely new double helix.
  * **Dispersive Model**: The parental DNA is cleaved into segments, and the resulting daughter molecules are a mixture of old and new interspersed segments.
  * **Semi-conservative Model**: Each parental strand separates and acts as a template for a new complementary strand, resulting in daughter molecules with one old and one new strand.

### Meselson and Stahl Experiment: Proof of Semi-conservative Replication

This experiment used **isotopes** (different forms of an element with different weights) of nitrogen to distinguish between parental and new DNA.

  * **Process**: Bacteria were first grown in **$^{15}N$ (heavy isotope)** and then transferred to a medium with **$^{14}N$ (light isotope)**.
  * **Centrifugation**: DNA was analyzed using a **CsCl (Cesium Chloride) gradient**, which separates DNA based on density.
  * **Findings**:
      * **Parental**: Showed one heavy $^{15}N$ band.
      * **1st Generation**: Showed one **hybrid band** (containing both $^{14}N$ and $^{15}N$), ruling out the conservative model.
      * **2nd Generation**: Showed two bands—one hybrid and one light ($^{14}N$), confirming the **semi-conservative model**.

### Summary Conclusions

  * Mendelian Laws established the basics of inheritance.
  * Morgan's experiments with *Drosophila melanogaster* (fruit flies) proved that chromosomes are the physical location of Mendel's heritable factors.
  * Classical experiments by Griffith, Hershey, Chase, Meselson, and Stahl proved that DNA is the heritable factor transferred across generations through semi-conservative replication.

Hershey and Chase labeled DNA with $^{32}P$ and protein with $^{35}S$; after infection and centrifugation, they found the $^{32}P$ (DNA) inside the bacterial pellet and the $^{35}S$ (protein) in the liquid supernatant. This proved that only DNA enters the bacteria to provide genetic instructions for new viruses.
2.  The transforming principle is the idea that a substance (now known as DNA) can be transferred from dead pathogenic bacteria (heat-killed S cells) to living nonpathogenic bacteria (R cells), permanently changing the living bacteria's traits to become pathogenic.
3.  In the conservative model, the original parental DNA strands stay together as a complete unit while serving as a template for a totally new molecule. In the semi-conservative model, the parental strands separate, and each individual strand serves as a template for a new complementary strand, resulting in daughter molecules that are half-old and half-new.
4.  The second replication showed two distinct bands: one hybrid ($^{14}N/^{15}N$) and one light ($^{14}N$). This confirmed the semi-conservative model because the dispersive model predicted that the DNA would continue to be a single, increasingly lighter hybrid mix rather than forming a pure light band.
5.  Adenine (A) always pairs with Thymine (T) via two hydrogen bonds, while Guanine (G) always pairs with Cytosine (C) via three hydrogen bonds.
