### Phylogeny of Prokaryotes

The document begins by comparing Bacteria, Archaea, and Eukarya through the study of evolutionary relationships, known as **phylogeny**:

  * **Nuclear Envelope:** Both Bacteria and Archaea lack a **nuclear envelope** (the membrane surrounding the genetic material), while it is present in Eukarya.
  * **Membrane-Enclosed Organelles:** Specialized internal structures like mitochondria are absent in Bacteria and Archaea but present in Eukarya.
  * **Cell Wall Composition:** Bacteria uniquely contain **peptidoglycan** (a network of sugar polymers cross-linked by polypeptides), which is absent in Archaea and Eukarya.
  * **Membrane Lipids:** Archaea possess unique **branched hydrocarbons** (complex fat molecules) in their membranes, whereas Bacteria and Eukarya use **unbranched hydrocarbons**.

### Characteristics of Bacteria

Bacteria are described as single-celled, microscopic organisms found on nearly every surface.

  * **Physical Shapes:** They exist in three primary forms: **Spherical** (**Cocci**), **Rod-shaped** (**Bacilli**), and **Spiral**.
  * **Metabolism:** Bacteria obtain energy in various ways:
      * **Photosynthetic cyanobacterium:** Bacteria that make their own food from sunlight.
      * **Scavengers:** Organisms that consume nutrients from their surroundings, such as harmless bacteria in the human gut.
      * **Pathogens:** Bacteria that attack other living organisms to cause infection.
  * **Motility:** Many bacteria move using a **flagella** (a tail-like appendage used for propulsion). They exhibit **taxis**, the ability to move toward or away from a stimulus, such as **chemotaxis** (movement in response to chemical signals).
  * **Reproduction:** Bacteria primarily reproduce through **binary fission**, an asexual process where a cell grows to twice its size and splits into two identical cells.

### Internal Organization and Genetic Diversity

  * **Genome:** The bacterial **genome** (complete set of genetic material) is circular and lacks the complex architecture found in eukaryotes. It is located in the **nucleoid region**.
  * **Plasmids:** These are small, circular, extra-chromosomal rings of DNA that can replicate independently.
  * **Sources of Variation:** Genetic diversity is driven by **rapid reproduction**, **mutation** (changes in DNA sequences), and **genetic recombination** (the combining of DNA from two different cells).

### Gram Staining and Cell Surface Structures

**Gram staining** is a laboratory technique used to classify bacteria based on their cell wall composition:

  * **Gram-positive:** These bacteria have a thick layer of peptidoglycan that traps **crystal violet stain**, making them appear purple or violet under a microscope.
  * **Gram-negative:** These possess a thinner peptidoglycan layer and an **outer membrane**. The initial stain rinses away, and they appear pink or red after a counterstain called **safranin** is applied.

### Antibiotics and Human Impact

Antibiotics are designed to target specific bacterial functions:

  * **Cell Wall Synthesis:** Inhibited by penicillin, cephalosporin, and vancomycin.
  * **Translation:** The process of making proteins, inhibited by tetracycline and erythromycin.
  * **DNA Replication:** Targeted by quinolones.
  * **Human Pathogens:** Examples include **Bacillus anthracis**, which causes Anthrax.
  * **Beneficial Roles:** Bacteria act as **decomposers** (breaking down dead matter), aid in the recycling of chemical elements, and are used in technology for **gene cloning** or producing **biodegradable plastics** (specifically **PHA**, polyhydroxyalkanoate).

### Characteristics of Viruses

Viruses are much smaller and simpler than bacteria and are considered **obligate intracellular parasites**, meaning they can only reproduce inside a host cell.

  * **Structure:** They consist of nucleic acid (DNA or RNA) enclosed in a protein shell called a **capsid**. Some have a **viral envelope** derived from host cell membranes.
  * **Bacteriophages:** These are viruses that specifically infect bacteria, such as E. coli.
  * **Large DNA Viruses:** Examples like the **Pandora Virus** (2.5 Mb genome) challenge the idea that all viruses are extremely small.
  * **Retroviruses:** Viruses like **HIV** use an enzyme called **reverse transcriptase** to copy their RNA genome into DNA, which then integrates into the host's genome as a **provirus**.

### Viral Diseases and Challenges

  * **Human Diseases:** Examples include **Poliomyelitis** (caused by poliovirus), **SARS** (caused by a coronavirus), and **Swine Flu** (H1N1 influenza).
  * **Classification of Influenza A:** Based on surface glycoproteins: **Hemagglutinin** (HA), which aids in receptor binding, and **Neuraminidase** (NA), which helps the virus release from infected cells.
  * **Evolution:** Viruses evolve through **reassortment**, where different viral strains swap genetic material to create novel viruses, such as the triple-reassortant H1N1.
  * **Future Challenges:** These include the risk of new viruses through reassortment, drug resistance, and the difficulty of mass-producing vaccines.

### Short Answer Questions

1.  The three factors contributing to genetic diversity in prokaryotes are rapid reproduction, mutation, and genetic recombination.
2.  Gram-positive bacteria have simpler cell walls with a thick layer of peptidoglycan, while Gram-negative bacteria have less peptidoglycan and an additional outer membrane.
3.  A provirus is viral DNA that has been integrated into a host's genome after being copied from the viral RNA genome by the enzyme reverse transcriptase.
4.  Prokaryotes are used in research for gene cloning (e.g., *E. coli*) and to produce transgenic plants (e.g., *Agrobacterium tumefaciens*).
5.  The H1N1 virus evolved through genetic reassortment, which is the swapping of genetic material between different avian, human, and swine viral strains.
