The lecture, titled "**Why Biology for Engineers: Part-II**," is part of a MOOC NPTEL course delivered by Prof. Sanjeeva Srivastava from IIT Bombay. It focuses on how bioengineering has initiated advancements in healthcare and technology through interdisciplinary collaboration.

### I. Bio-inspired Engineering

This field involves studying nature’s elements to develop designs and models for solving complex human problems.

  * **Waterproofing (Lotus Effect)**: The lotus leaf has microscopic, rough surfaces that trap air, allowing water droplets to float off. This observation led to the creation of multi-functional textile finishes and paints that are water-repellent and stain-resistant.
  * **Architecture (Termite Mounds)**: Termite mounds maintain stable internal temperatures through **passive cooling**. This inspired the air conditioning system of the Eastgate Building in Zimbabwe, which uses only 10% of the energy required by conventional buildings for ventilation.
  * **Tsunami Alert System (Dolphins)**: Dolphins can recognize calls from 25 kilometers away despite sound-scattering in water. This principle was used to develop high-performance underwater sonar and modems currently used in Tsunami early warning systems in the Indian Ocean.
  * **Transportation (Kingfisher)**: The beak of a Kingfisher, which allows it to dive silently into water, inspired the front end of the Shinkansen bullet train in Japan. This resulted in a quieter train that travels faster and uses less electricity.
  * **Fog Harvesting (Namib Desert Beetle)**: The *Stenocara gracilipes* beetle collects morning fog on its back and rolls the moisture into its mouth. An IIT Bombay alumnus developed fence-like meshes that mimic this to collect drinking water for villagers in desert areas.

### III. Revolution in Medical Research

Medical practices are shifting from standard biochemical testing to gene and protein-based technologies.

  * **Personalized Omics Profiling**: This analyzes an individual's DNA, RNA, and proteins using **genomics**, **transcriptomics**, and **proteomics** to provide unique health information.
  * **Success Case**: Dr. Lukas Wartman treated his leukemia by identifying a specific **FLT3 mutation**, allowing him to use a targeted inhibitor (*sunitinib*) originally approved for kidney cancer.
  * **Human Phenome Project**: Based in London, this project profiles all **metabolites** in healthy and diseased populations to understand metabolic networks using mass spectrometry and NMR technology.
  * **Systems Biology**: An emerging field that integrates clinical information with data from the genome, transcriptome, proteome, and phenome. This "Big Data" approach requires computational engineers to build models and simulations to predict biological behavior.

### IV. Bio-Research at IIT Bombay

Bio-research is a reality at IIT Bombay, with 15% of the faculty involved across various departments like Mechanical, Electrical, and Computer Science.

  * **Healthcare Research Consortium**: A joint initiative between IIT Bombay, local hospitals (like TMC and KEM), and pharma companies.
  * **Ongoing Projects**:
      * Mobile-based portable diagnostic systems for urine and blood sugar analysis.
      * Prosthesis devices, such as modular knee joint implants designed for Indian patients.
      * Mobile platforms to detect sickle cells in blood at the **point-of-care**.
  * **Alumni Impact**: Engineers from IIT Bombay, such as Dr. Harmit Malik (Chemical Engineering) and Dr. Vivek Jayaraman (Aerospace Engineering), have successfully transitioned into leading biological research globally.
