### **Basics and Terminology**

The lecture begins by establishing essential definitions for understanding genetics:

  * **Genotype:** This refers to an organism's full hereditary information or its complete genetic contents.
  * **Phenotype:** These are the actual observed properties of an individual, such as their physical appearance, morphology, or characteristics.
  * **Gene:** Formerly referred to by Mendel as "heritable factors," a gene is a specific sequence of nucleotides located at a particular place (locus) along a chromosome that contains information passed from one generation to the next.
  * **Allele:** These are alternative variations or different forms of a single gene. Each organism carries two copies of a gene, with one allele inherited from each parent.
      * **Dominant Allele:** An allele that shows its effect even if only one copy is present (e.g., the 'P' allele for purple flowers).
      * **Recessive Allele:** An allele whose effect is masked by a dominant allele and only appears when two copies are present (e.g., the 'p' allele for white flowers).
  * **Homozygous:** Having two identical alleles for a particular gene (e.g., PP or pp).
  * **Heterozygous:** Having two different alleles for a particular gene (e.g., Pp).
  * **Punnett Square:** A tool used in genetics to predict the possible genotypes and phenotypes of offspring from a particular cross.
  * **Traits:** Specific variants of a character, such as purple or white flower color.

The lecture illustrates the impact of alleles through the example of flower color. A single base pair change in a DNA sequence (e.g., from C to A) can determine whether a plant produces an enzyme for purple pigment or lacks it, resulting in white flowers.

### **Gregor Mendel and His Experiments**

Mendel, an Augustinian monk with an interest in physics and chemistry, discovered the basic principles of heredity by meticulously breeding garden peas. He chose pea plants as his experimental system because:

  * They are available in many variations.
  * They have a short generation time.
  * They produce a large number of offspring, allowing for statistical analysis.
  * Their reproductive organs (stamens for pollen, carpels for eggs) are well-separated, making controlled cross-pollination easier.

Mendel focused on **true-breeding varieties**, which are plants that consistently produce offspring with the same traits over multiple generations when self-pollinated. He studied seven distinct characters with alternative forms, such as flower color (purple vs. white), seed shape (round vs. wrinkled), and stem length (tall vs. dwarf).

### **Law of Segregation**

Mendel's first law, the **Law of Segregation**, was derived from **monohybrid crosses** (studying one trait at a time).

  * **Parental (P) Generation:** Mendel crossed two true-breeding varieties (e.g., PP for purple and pp for white).
  * **F1 (First Filial) Generation:** The resulting hybrid offspring all displayed the dominant trait (e.g., purple flowers, genotype Pp).
  * **F2 (Second Filial) Generation:** When F1 hybrids self-pollinated or cross-pollinated, the recessive trait reappeared. The observed **phenotypic ratio** was approximately **3:1** (dominant to recessive), while the **genotypic ratio** was **1:2:1** (1 homozygous dominant: 2 heterozygous: 1 homozygous recessive).

The law states that the two alleles for a heritable trait separate (segregate) during gamete formation, so each gamete carries only one allele for each gene.

#### **The Test Cross**

To determine whether an organism showing a dominant phenotype is homozygous (PP) or heterozygous (Pp), Mendel used a **test cross**. This involves breeding the unknown individual with a **homozygous recessive** (pp) individual.

  * If all offspring are purple, the unknown parent was homozygous dominant (PP).
  * If offspring show a 1:1 ratio of purple to white, the unknown parent was heterozygous (Pp).

### **Law of Independent Assortment**

Mendel's second law, the **Law of Independent Assortment**, was formulated by studying two characters simultaneously in a **dihybrid cross**. For example, he crossed plants having yellow, round seeds (YYRR) with those having green, wrinkled seeds (yyrr).

The resulting F1 generation was YyRr. Mendel tested two hypotheses:

1.  **Dependent Assortment:** If traits were inherited together, the F2 phenotypic ratio would be 3:1.
2.  **Independent Assortment:** If each pair of alleles segregated independently, the F2 generation would show four different phenotypes in a **9:3:3:1 ratio**.

Mendel's results confirmed independent assortment, showing that each pair of alleles segregates independently of any other pair during gamete formation.

### **Rediscovery of Mendelism**

Mendel published his findings in 1866, but they remained largely unnoticed by the scientific community. It wasn't until 1900 that three scientists—**Hugo de Vries, Erich Tschermak, and Carl Correns**—independently rediscovered these same laws through their own research, leading to the belated recognition of Mendel as the "father of genetics".

#### **Short Answer Questions Model Answers**

1.  **Definition of Allele:** Alleles are alternative variations or different forms of a gene that account for variations in inherited characters. Each organism harbors two copies of a gene, one inherited from each parent.
2.  **True-breeding Varieties:** True-breeding varieties are plants that, when self-pollinated, always produce offspring with the same traits over multiple generations, such as purple flowers always producing purple flowers. They were important because they ensured Mendel was starting with homozygous organisms, providing a consistent baseline for his crosses.
3.  **Particulate vs. Blending Hypothesis:** The particulate hypothesis suggests that discrete heritable units (genes) are passed along and shuffled like a deck of cards, remaining unchanged from generation to generation. The blending hypothesis proposed that genetic material from two parents mixes like paint, such as blue and yellow blending to create green.
4.  **Pea Plant as an Experimental System:** Pea plants were ideal because they were available in many variations, had a short generation time, and produced a large number of offspring. Additionally, Mendel could strictly control mating through easy cross-pollination or self-pollination.
5.  **Law of Independent Assortment:** This law concludes that each pair of alleles segregates independently of any other pair of alleles during gamete formation. This occurs when studying two or more characters (genes) simultaneously, provided they are not linked.
