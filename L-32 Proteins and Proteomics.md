This document, titled **"Proteins & Proteomics"** (Lecture 32 of the course *Bioengineering: An Interface with Biology and Medicine*), explores the transition from genome sequencing to the large-scale study of proteins. Its primary purpose is to explain the field of **proteomics**—the study of all proteins expressed by a genome—and the various technologies used to analyze their properties, functions, and interactions.

### 1\. From Genomics to Proteomics

The document begins by contextualizing proteomics following the success of various **genome sequencing projects**.

  * **Human Genome Project (1990–2003)**: A collaborative international effort to map the 3 billion bases of human DNA. While it identified approximately 20,500 protein-coding genes, it revealed that gene number does not correlate directly with **biological complexity**.
  * **Biological Complexity**: Organisms like the fruit fly (*Drosophila melanogaster*) have 14,000 genes, while humans have roughly 20,500. The higher complexity of humans is better explained by the **proteome** rather than the genome.
  * **The Central Dogma Expansion**:
      * **Genome**: The static snapshot of total encoded information (\~20,000 genes).
      * **Transcriptome**: All RNA transcripts produced (\~100,000 transcripts).
      * **Proteome**: The complete set of proteins expressed by a genome (\>1,000,000 proteins).
  * **Diversity Mechanisms**: A single gene can produce multiple proteins through **alternative splicing** (combining coding sequences, or **exons**, in different ways) and **post-translational modifications (PTMs)** (chemical changes after protein synthesis like **phosphorylation** or **glycosylation**).

### 2\. The Field of Proteomics

Proteomics provides an integrated view of cellular processes by studying several key protein properties.

  * **Core Objectives**: Proteomics aims to study the extent of protein expression, PTMs, enzymatic regulation, **localization** (where a protein is in the cell), and **intermolecular interactions** (how proteins talk to each other).
  * **Proteome Maps**: The first draft maps of the human proteome were reported in 2014–2015, identifying about 17,500 proteins, though the work remains incomplete.

### 3\. Proteomic Technologies and Analysis

The document outlines four major modules or technological streams for proteome analysis.

  * **Module I: Gel-based Proteomics**:
      * **2-D Electrophoresis**: Separates proteins based on two properties: **isoelectric point (pI)** (the pH at which a protein has no net charge) and **molecular weight (MW)**.
      * **DIGE (Difference Gel Electrophoresis)**: Uses fluorescent **cyanine dyes** (Cy2, Cy3, Cy5) to label different samples, allowing for the precise quantification of proteins between control and test conditions on a single gel.
  * **Module II: Mass Spectrometry (MS)**:
      * **Function**: MS identifies and quantifies thousands of proteins by sorting ions based on their **mass-to-charge ratio (m/z)**.
      * **Ionization**: "Soft" techniques like **MALDI** (Matrix-Assisted Laser Desorption Ionization) and **ESI** (Electrospray Ionization) convert proteins into gas-phase ions without destroying them.
      * **Analyzers**: Components like **TOF** (Time of Flight) and **Quadrupole (Q)** measure these ions.
  * **Module III: Interactomics**:
      * **Definition**: The study of protein-protein or other biomolecular interactions.
      * **Protein Microarrays**: "Bait" proteins are immobilized on a surface and probed with "query" proteins to see which ones bind.
  * **Module IV: Label-free Detection**:
      * Uses technologies like **Surface Plasmon Resonance (SPR)** to study real-time binding kinetics (e.g., drug-protein interactions) without using fluorescent or radioactive labels.

### 4\. Integrated Omics and Systems Biology

The document concludes that understanding physiology and medicine requires an **Integrated Omics** approach. This involves combining data from the **Genome**, **Transcriptome**, **Proteome**, **Metabolome** (all metabolites), and **Phenome** (all physical traits) to investigate biological systems comprehensively. Such integration helps identify molecular mechanisms of diseases and aids in developing personalized therapies.
